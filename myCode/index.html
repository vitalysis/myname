    Breast Lumps: Causes & Symptoms

    Overview

    Finding a lump in your breast can be very frightening, but not every lump means cancer. A breast lump is a localized swelling, mass, or growth, that suddenly develops in the breast and feels different from the surrounding tissue. It can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). While many breast lumps are harmless, it is important to assess changes in the lump. Breast lumps are not breast cancers.

    Most breast lumps are often benign and non-cancerous. Fibroadenoma is a common type of breast lump found in young women that is usually mobile, smooth, and firm. Lumps have different forms, which can also be associated with different colors of nipple discharge, sensation (painful or painless), and mobility (movable or immovable). Understanding this at an early stage is important. Breast lumps can be treated differently depending on the cause. Some lumps do not necessarily need treatment.

    How Should a Normal Breast Feel?

    It is important to be familiar with how your breasts normally feel in order to detect any new changes. It is your body, and you can often notice changes best. Regular self-examination can help you detect changes early. Knowing how your breasts normally feel can also help you describe any changes clearly to your doctor. Although breast examination can sometimes cause worry, especially when a change is noticed, many breast lumps are non-cancerous. Normal breasts often feel lumpy and vary in consistency from woman to woman. Normal breast changes can occur at different phases of life, such as puberty, pregnancy, and menopause. Changes can also happen with weight loss, which may reduce breast size, and with weight gain, which may increase breast size. Most normal changes usually occur in both breasts simultaneously and roughly symmetrically, meaning in the same relative position. Such changes are less likely to indicate a problem than those occurring in only one breast.

    How Does a Lump In The Breast Feel?

    A breast lump may feel like a small rock (hard) or feel spongy, like berries surrounded by other breast tissue. The lump will feel different from the surrounding tissue. Some lumps may feel painful when pressed, while others may be painless even when squeezed. It is important to examine your breasts regularly. If you notice symptoms like these, do not panic. Out of every 10 cases of breast lumps, about 8 are non-cancerous.

    When To See A Doctor

    • Discharge from nipple
    • A newly discovered hard lump
    • A lump that does not go away after menstruation
    • Skin changes such as redness
    • A lump that grows larger or changes
    • Nipple change - e.g., a scaling rash or change in shape

    What To Expect From Your Doctor?

    When you see your doctor regarding a breast lump, a few questions will be asked, such as when the lump was discovered and whether there are other symptoms. A physical examination will be carried out immediately. If the cause cannot yet be determined, other tests may be required:

    Mammogram

    A mammogram is an X-ray of the breast that helps identify breast abnormalities.

    Ultrasound

    This is an imaging test that uses sound waves to create pictures inside the breast.

    Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

    This is another imaging test that uses a magnetic field and radio waves to take detailed pictures of the breast.

    Biopsy

    Biopsy is a procedure to remove cells, tissue, or fluid for medical examination, usually done in a histology laboratory. Biopsies are carried out when areas of concern are identified, to help diagnose or prevent certain chronic conditions. There are different types of breast biopsies, such as surgical biopsy (excisional or incisional biopsy), fine-needle aspiration, core needle biopsy, and others.

    Fine-Needle Aspiration

    Also called fine-needle biopsy, this involves the use of a needle to remove fluid from a breast lump. Ultrasound is sometimes used to guide the needle. Benign cysts tend to go away after the fluid is removed. If the fluid appears cloudy or bloody, the sample will be sent to the laboratory for analysis to check for cancer cells.

    Types Of Breast Lumps

    There are different types of breast lumps, but they are broadly classified as benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). However, benign breast lumps are more common than malignant lumps. It is important to see a healthcare professional to evaluate any breast lumps when one is noticed.

    Benign Breast Lumps

    Benign breast lumps are growths or masses that are non-cancerous. These are common breast lumps that can change with factors such as hormones, pregnancy, or breastfeeding.
    There are many types and causes of benign breast lumps (non-cancerous), including:


    • Breast cyst
    • Fibroadenoma
    • Abscess
    • Lipoma
    • Intraductal papilloma
    • Breast infection

    Malignant Breast Lumps

    A malignant breast lump is a cancerous lump that is usually hard, painless, and has an irregular shape.

    • Breast cancer

    Causes Of Breast Lump

    Breast lumps can occur in both women and men, but are much more common in women. They can be caused by various factors, including:

    • Benign growths

    • Fibroadenomas:

      • These breast lumps are a common type of benign (non-cancerous) lump. They move easily under the skin when touched and may grow larger over time.

    • Breast Cysts:

      • Breast cysts are smooth, non-cancerous, fluid-filled sacs in the breast. They commonly affect younger women and are less common in women after menopause.

    • Abscesses:

      • This lump can develop in the breast, sometimes during breastfeeding. A breast abscess is usually caused by an untreated infection called mastitis. Breast abscesses can be painful and may appear as a red, swollen lump in the breast. Discharge such as pus may drain out if there is an opening on the skin.

    • Fibrocystic Changes:

      • These are small, fluid-filled sacs and rubbery-like tissue areas that can be felt as lumps in the breast.

    • Breast Cancer:

      • This is typically a hard, irregular-shaped, painless mass that feels different from surrounding breast tissue. There may be changes in the nipple, such as bloody discharge, especially if one nipple suddenly looks different from the other. Changes in the shape of the breast may also occur.

    • Breast Infection:

      • Infection in the breast tissue can cause a localized area of hardened tissue.

    • Lipoma:

      • Lipoma is a lump that grows under the skin and is caused by an overgrowth of fat cells. They are usually soft and harmless.

    • Intraductal papilloma:

      • An intraductal papilloma is a lump that develops in the milk ducts of the breast. It is most common among middle-aged women and usually occurs close to the nipple. This lump can cause a blood-stained discharge from the nipple.

    Breast Lump In Males

    Males can also develop breast lumps for reasons similar to females. In a few cases, the lump may indicate breast cancer in males, although the chance of a cancerous lump is lower in men.
    Generally, breast lumps are less common in men. However, although they are rare, they can still be caused by a variety of conditions, including:

    Gynecomastia

    It is the most common breast condition in males. Gynecomastia occurs when breast tissue enlarges due to certain health conditions, medications, or hormonal imbalance. A small growth under the nipple can be felt or sometimes seen.

    Cyst

    Although breast cysts are rare in men and much more common in women, they are usually benign in men.

    Papillomas

    These are also very rare and non-cancerous growths that develop in the milk ducts of the breast tissue. They can cause clear or bloody nipple discharge.

    Fat Necrosis

    Fat necrosis is damage to the fatty tissues after trauma. It may appear similar to cancer on imaging tests due to its firmness.

    Lipoma

    Lipoma is a movable benign fatty lump that is usually soft and can slowly grow in the breast.

    Treatment For Breast Lumps

    Your doctor should determine the cause of your breast lump to formulate an appropriate treatment plan. However, not all breast lumps need treatment, as some can disappear on their own over time.
    The type of lump determines the type of treatment:

    • If it is a breast infection, your doctor may prescribe antibiotics for treatment.

    • For a breast cyst, fluid drainage may be required.

    • Biopsy may be needed to characterize the mass (if there is suspicion of cancer, if the lump is growing, or if it is painful).

    • Treatment for cancer, if a biopsy confirms breast cancer. Therapies may include surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy.

    Most breast lumps are harmless, but every new lump should be checked by a health professional. Early detection can save a life.


    NOTE: This article is for educational purposes only and should not replace professional medical advice or diagnosis. If you find a lump or any change in your breast, please see a qualified health provider.
